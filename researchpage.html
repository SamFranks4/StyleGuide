Samuel Franks Writing Style Guide

(undergraduate academic voice – tailored for you, Sam)

Overview

This guide captures the distinctive writing voice of Samuel Franks, based on your style preferences and writing habits across papers. The guide is especially suited for longer research and academic essays.

Tone and Personality

Core Voice Characteristics

  • Earnest and straightforward: Shows engagement with the topic without irony or showmanship.
  • Confident but measured: Present claims assertively, yet admit complexity and limits of knowledge.
  • Conversational-academic: Balance formal academic requirements with language that still sounds like you.
  • Personally invested: Bring personal experience into analysis (e.g., video games sharpening reflexes).
  • Faith/value-neutral unless assignment asks for faith.

Emotional Register

  • Maintain a professional tone while letting convictions appear ("I think", "I believe").
  • Show intellectual humility: “there are some outliers” or “it’s complicated”.
  • Show concern when addressing issues (e.g., generational effects of gaming, societal issues).

Sentence Structure

Sentence Length Patterns

  • Average sentence length: long — many sentences are run-on, complex.
  • Mix: Some very long sentences, some shorter for effect.
  • Rhythm: Typically one long explanatory sentence, then a shorter emphatic one, then a transition.

Common Sentence Patterns

  • Opening statements: “One crucial aspect of [topic] is [specific element]”, “[Topic] has always been an issue in [context], and with [circumstance], it is hard to [action].”, “[Stat] shows… and [related stat] reveals…”
  • Explanatory sequences: “This means that [explanation]. [Consequence]. [Further implication].”, “When [condition], [result], so [implication].”, “Although [concession], [main point].”
  • Transitions: “One thing that [verb] is [subject].”, “Another aspect is [point].”, “However, [contrast].”
  • Emphatic conclusions: “This should [action].”, “Ultimately, [conclusive statement].”, “Overall, [summary point].”

Syntactic Preferences

  • Use subordinate clauses to add nuance.
  • Occasionally break parallelism for emphasis.
  • Sentence starters: varied; comfortable with “Although”, “When”, “One thing that…”.
  • Passive voice: sparing; prefer active voice.

Paragraph Structure

Paragraph Length

  • Body paragraphs: ~8-12 sentences (~150-250 words).
  • Introduction paragraphs: ~6-8 sentences, broad to specific.
  • Conclusion paragraphs: ~5-7 sentences, shorter and punchy.

Paragraph Organization: Standard Body Paragraph

  • Topic sentence (often “One aspect…”).
  • Context/explanation (1-2 sentences).
  • Evidence/support (2-4 sentences).
  • Analysis (2-3 sentences).
  • Transition or mini-conclusion (connects to next paragraph).

Transition Strategies

  • Between paragraphs: echo key word from previous + new direction.
  • Within paragraphs: “However,” “This means that,” “Another” commonly used.
  • Section shifts: Can be abrupt when needed.

Common Phrases and Expressions

  • “One thing that…”
  • “Another aspect is…”
  • “This means that…”
  • “However, …”
  • “Although [concession], [main point]”
  • “Oftentimes…”
  • “All of these [elements] contribute to…”
  • “Overall, …”
  • “Ultimately, …”
  • “In order to [goal], [action] will need to [requirement].”

Evaluative & Analytical Phrases

  • “It’s important to note that…”
  • “One thing I understand better…”
  • “This will affect the way…”
  • “I would say…”
  • “Generally, most [group] agree…”
  • “There are some outliers…”
  • “After researching [topic], I have gained…”
  • “One crucial aspect of…”

Personal Reflection Phrases

  • “As a [student/person] I…”
  • “In my experience…”
  • “I feel like…”
  • “I think that…”
  • “I believe…”
  • “This has led me to believe…”

Hedging & Qualification

  • “Most likely”
  • “Probably”
  • “Oftentimes”
  • “Generally”
  • “It seems”
  • “Might/could/would”
  • “In some cases”

Word Choice & Vocabulary

  • Accessible, direct language; avoid jargon overload.
  • Use discipline-specific terms when appropriate.
  • Avoid over-complex words when simpler ones suffice.
  • Characteristic words: “Crucial”, “Oftentimes”, “Adverse”, “Aspects”, “Contributes to”, “Leads to”, “Overall”, “Ultimately”.
  • Avoid repetition; vary phrasing for “One aspect”, “This means that”, “Oftentimes”.

Formatting Preferences

  • Header: Name, Professor, Course, Date (day Month year).
  • Title: Centered, title case, not bolded.
  • Spacing: Double-spaced.
  • Indentation: First line of each paragraph indented.
  • Margins: 1 inch.
  • Font: Times New Roman 12 pt.
  • Citation Style: MLA, in-text parenthetical, Works Cited separate page.
  • Never use em dashes.

Quotation Integration

  • Use “According to [Author]” or “[Source] states…” before quoting.
  • Short quotes: integrate with signal phrase.
  • Block quotes: avoid; prefer shorter integrated quotes.
  • Explain significance after evidence: “This means that…”

Argumentation & Rhetorical Strategies

  • Thesis: End of introduction, complex sentence outlining 2-3 main supporting points.
  • Evidence: Statistics, expert testimony, studies, personal experience; blend types.
  • Counterargument: Usually before conclusion; state fairly, then rebut.
  • Persuasive techniques: logical primary, ethical through experience, subtle emotional.
  • Audience: Undergraduate professor, college-level general knowledge; define terms when needed.

Revision Checklist

  • Voice & Tone: sincere, engaged, “I” when appropriate, confident with humility.
  • Structure: thesis at end of intro, body paragraphs ~8-12 sentences, counterargument penultimate paragraph, conclusion synthesizes.
  • Sentence-Level: variety, mix simple/complex, use key phrases moderately, subordinate clauses, moderate run-ons.
  • Word Choice: accessible vocabulary, avoid repetition, mostly active voice.
  • Evidence & Citation: signal phrases, proper MLA, explain evidence, personal experience when relevant.
  • Formatting: header, title centered, double-spaced, indent first line, Works Cited separate page, font Times New Roman 12 pt, 1-inch margins, never use em dashes.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Starting too many paragraphs with “One aspect”.
  • Repeating “oftentimes”.
  • Too many long or run-on sentences without breaks.
  • Layering in too much jargon.
  • Leaving personal anecdotes disconnected from analysis.
  • Over-relying on “This means that”.

Final Notes

  • Strengths: voice is genuine, personally relevant, balanced formal/conversational tone.
  • Areas for growth: manage run-ons, vary transitional starters, tie anecdotes to analysis, balance evidence and personal voice.
  • Evolution: your voice is maturing; core characteristics remain strong.
